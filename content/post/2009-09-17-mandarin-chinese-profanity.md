### Turtles and eggs




The 中文大辭典_Zhōng wén dà cí diǎn (Encyclopedic Dictionary of the Chinese Language))_(something a little like the OED), discusses 王八 (wáng bā) in vol. 6 p. 281. "Wáng bā" is the term that is usually written casually for the slur that means something like "son of a bitch." "Wáng bā 王八" originally got switched over from another "忘八 wàng bā" (one that referred to any very unvirtuous individual) because of a nasty piece of work with the family name Wáng 王 who picked up the nickname 賊王八 zéi Wáng bā ("the thieving Wang Eight") but for being a dastard, not for being a bastard. The dictionary doesn't say, but he may have been the eighth Wang among his siblings. Anyway, he became "crook Wang eight" and the term stuck and spread just as "[Maverick](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Maverick)" did in English. There is a pun here because of the earlier expression 忘八 wáng bā used to describe (1) any person who forgets/disregards the eight virtues, (2) an un-virtuous woman, i.e., one who sleeps around. The first meaning applied to the dastardly Wang, but the family name got "stuck" to the second, sexual, term.




A "wángbādàn 忘/王八蛋" is the offspring "laid" by the unvirtuous woman. Why "laid"? Another meaning of 王八 is[鼈](http://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E9%BC%88)biē, fresh-water turtle.[4]Turtle heads reemerging from hiding in the turtle's shell look like the[glans](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glans)emerging from the[foreskin](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foreskin), and turtles lay eggs. So a "wang ba" is a woman who has lost her virtue, and a "wang ba dan" is the progeny of such a woman, a turtle product, but, figuratively, also a penis product.

### Age





* **lǎo bù sǐde**老不死的 -- death grip on life -- is used as an angry comment directed against old people who refuse to die and so clog up the ladder to promotion in some organization. The expression comes from the_Analects of Confucius_where the Master complains against those who engage in heterodox practices aimed at assuring them extreme longevity. In the original these individuals are described as "lǎo ér bù sǐ 老而不死," i.e., it it said that they "are old and yet they (will not=) refuse to die."


* **lǎo tóuzi**老頭子,literally "old head," it refers in a somewhat slighting way to old men. Its usage is rather like such expressions as "old gaffer," "old geezer," etc. in English.


* **xiǎo guǐ**小鬼," little devils," is used familiarly and (usually) affectionately.

## Racism




As in any language, Chinese has specific terms and racial slurs for different ethnicities, governments and backgrounds.





### Against westerners





* **yáng guǐzi**([Chinese](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language):洋鬼子) "Foreign devil", a slur for[White people](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_people)
* **guǐlǎo**([Chinese](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language):鬼佬) Borrowed from Cantonese "[Gweilo](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gweilo)", "ghost" or "ghost guy", a slur for white people
* **hóng máo guǐzi**([Chinese](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language):红毛鬼子) "Red hair devil", rude slang term for Caucasians, especially Anglos
* **máo zi**([Chinese](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language):毛子) Ethnic slur against[Russians](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russians). (Literally "hairs".) Alternatively 红毛子 (hóng máo zi, red (communist) hairs), 俄毛子 (é máo zi,[Rus](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rus)hairs). Similar concept to "hóng máo guǐzi" above.
* **lǎo wài**([Chinese](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language):老外) "foreigner", literally "old outsider", slang term for Caucasians in Mainland China, especially Anglos. Since this term is quite often used colloquially without malicious intent (even directly to foreigners proficient in Mandarin), its meaning is highly context specific.



### Against Japanese





* **xiǎo Rìběn**(小日本)="Japs" — Literally "little Japan"(ese). This term is so common that it has very little impact left (Google Search returns 21,000,000 results as of August 2007). The term can be used to refer to either Japan or individual Japanese. "小", or the word "little", is usually construed as "puny", "lowly" or "small country", but not "spunky".
* 日本鬼子 (Rìběn guǐzi) — Literally "Japanese devils". This is used mostly in the context of the[Second Sino-Japanese War](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War), when Japan invaded and occupied large areas of China. This is the title of[a Japanese documentary on Japanese war crimes during WWII](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_Devils).
* [倭](http://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%80%AD)(Wō) — This was an ancient[Chinese](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language)name for Japan, but was also adopted by the Japanese. Today, its usage in Chinese is usually intended to give a negative connotation (see Wōkòu below). The character is said to also mean "_dwarf_", although that meaning was not apparent when the name was first used. See[Wa (Japan)](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wa_(Japan)).
* 倭寇 (Wōkòu) — Originally referred to Japanese pirates and armed sea merchants who raided the Chinese coastline during the[Ming Dynasty](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ming_Dynasty)(see[Wokou](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wokou)). The term was adopted during the[Second Sino-Japanese War](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Sino-Japanese_War)to refer to invading Japanese forces, (similarly to[Germans](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany)being called[Huns](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hun)). The word is today sometimes used to refer to all Japanese people in extremely negative contexts.
* 日本狗 (Rìběn gǒu, Cantonese: Yat Boon Gau) — Literally "Japanese dogs". The word is used to refer to all Japanese people in extremely negative contexts.
* 大腳盆族 (dà jiǎo pén zú) - Ethnic slur towards Japanese used predominantly by Northern Chinese, mainly those from the city of[Tianjin](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tianjin). Literally "Big Feet Bowl Race".
* 黃軍 (huáng jūn) - Literally "Yellow Soldier(s)", used during World War II to represent[Imperial Japanese soldiers](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army)due to the colour of the uniform. Today, it is used negatively against all Japanese. Since the stereotype of Japanese soldiers are commonly portrayed in war-related TV series in China as short men, with a toothbrush moustache (and sometimes round glasses, in the case of higher ranks), 黃軍 is also often used to pull jokes on Chinese people with these characteristics, and thus "appear like" Japanese soldiers.
* 自慰队 (zì wèi duì) - A pun on the homophone "自卫队" (zì wèi duì, literally "Self-Defence Forces", see[Japan Self-Defense Forces](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan_Self-Defense_Forces)), the definition of 慰 (wèi) used is "to comfort". This phrase is used to refer to Japanese (whose military force is known as "自卫队") being[stereotypically hypersexual](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereotypes_of_East_Asians#Predators_of_white_women), as "自慰队" means "Self-comforting Forces", referring to[masturbation](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masturbation).



### Against Koreans





* 高丽棒子 (Gāolì bàng zǐ) - Derogatory term used against all ethnic Koreans. 高丽 ([Traditional](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_Chinese_character): 高麗) refers to Ancient Korea ([Koryo](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koryo)), while 棒子 means_"club"_or_"corncob"_, referring to how Koreans would fit into trousers of the Ancient Koryo design. Sometimes 韓棒子 (hán bàng zǐ, "韓" referring to[South Korea](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Korea)) is also used.
* 死棒子 (sǐ bàng zǐ) - Literally "dead club" or "dead corncob"; refer to 高丽棒子 above.
* 二鬼子 (èr guǐ zǐ) - (See[日本鬼子](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Japanese_sentiment#In_Chinese)) During[World War II](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II), 二鬼子 referred to[hanjian](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanjian)and[Koreans](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chosen_Army_of_Japan)in the[Imperial Japanese Army](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Japanese_Army), as the Japanese were known as "鬼子"_(devils)_. 二鬼子 literally means_"second devils"_. Today, 二鬼子 is used against all ethnic Koreans. This is also due to Koreans and Japanese being similar in appearance to Japanese, and many Chinese believing that Koreans are copying Japanese culture.



### Other





* 印度阿三 (Yìndù ā sān) - Ethnic slur against[Indians](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India). "A" is a[vocative](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocative), and "san" is three, so "a san" means the third son or third child in a family. So Indians are assigned third-class status by this locution.
* 阿差 (ā chā) - Similar to the above, this ethnic slur is common among the Cantonese speaking crowd especially those from Hong Kong and Southeast Asia. * 台巴子 (tái bāzi) - Slur originating from the city of[Shanghai](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shanghai), 台巴子 refers to Taiwanese, especially advocates of[Taiwan independence](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan_independence). "Bazi" can mean a clitoris or (in baby-talk) a "wee-wee" (the penis of a little boy).
* 共匪 (gòngfei) - Literally "Communist Bandits" referring to Mainlanders. The term has been in use since the[Chinese Civil War](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Civil_War)by the[Kuomintang](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuomintang)against the[Chinese Communist Party](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_Communist_Party), however today reflects the rifts in cross-strait relations.
* 老黑 (lǎo hēi) - Literally "Old Black", Anti-African racial slur. Essentially the same as coon, porch monkey, or jungle bunny.
* 黑鬼子 (hēi guǐzi) - Literally "Black devil", Anti-African/black people slur similar to[nigger](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigger).
* 黑鬼 (hēi guǐ) - Same as 黑鬼子.
* 印泥巴 or 印尼巴 (both**yìn ní ba**) - a play on "印尼" (Indonesia) and "泥巴" (mud), where 尼/泥 are homophones, thus paralleling Indonesians with dirtiness.

**Bōlí**([玻璃](http://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E7%8E%BB%E7%92%83), glass)-- lit., "glass" person. It comes from a passage in the_Dream of the Red Chamber_in which Phoenix is described as having a "crystal heart in a glass body," meaning that she was glistening, pure, clear, fastidious, etc. It stands as high praise for a lady, but sounds too feminine for a (stereotypical) male. The English translation of Bai Xian-yong's novel about male homosexuals in Taiwan includes the term "crystal boys," derived from the same passage in the earlier novel, and also a rather gruff reference to the old photographer who befriends some of the boys as "you old glass," which, delivered by a female friend of his, comes out sounding about on the level of "you old fart," i.e., not really so very offensive, but indicating a passing mood of aggravation on the speaker's part. Nevertheless, the general meaning is probably closer to "old queer."




**Nán fēng**(男風, male custom, is homophonous with (南風, southern custom.) The first writing of the term would fairly easily be picked out as referring to sexual interactions, whereas the second term could just mean "the customs of the southern part of China." Perhaps because male sexual arousal is easier to spot where heavy clothing is not worn, or perhaps simply because of the frequent use of this term, homosexuality came to be regarded as more common and accepted in the southern part of China.




**[Tóngzhì](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tongzhi)**(同志) (lit. "[comrade](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comrade)") was recently adopted in Hong Kong and Taiwan to mean homosexual, and is frequently used on the mainland. Literally the term means "one having same aspirations," and was transferred from the arena of political allegiances to the realm of sexual alliances.
